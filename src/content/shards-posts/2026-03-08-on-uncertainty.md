title: On uncertainty as a public virtue

The most underrated political skill is the ability to hold a position
lightly. Not weakly — lightly. To be able to say "this is what I currently
think, and here is what would change my mind" is harder than it sounds, and
rarer than it should be.

We tend to read certainty as strength, and doubt as a defect of character.
Both readings are wrong. Certainty without justification is just volume.
Doubt with reasons is the substance of a working democracy: the willingness
to be the kind of person whose views could, in principle, be moved by
evidence.

This is not centrism. A person can hold a strong view and still be open to
its revision. The point is not where the view sits on a spectrum but how
it is held. The question to ask of any position — yours or someone else's
— is: what is the smallest fact that would make you let go of this?
