When pop-science says "everything is made of particles," it is using a
word modern physics has quietly demoted. The current best account is that
the world is made of *fields* — continuous, space-filling somethings
whose ripples we call particles when we squint.

The shift sounds technical but the consequences are philosophical. The
universe stops being a stage on which discrete objects are arranged and
becomes a single continuous medium that occasionally vibrates in
particle-shaped ways. The electron is not a tiny ball; it is a stable
pattern of excitation in the electron field, which is everywhere.

This buys us things. It explains why every electron is exactly identical:
they are not copies, they are the same pattern repeated by the same field.
It also explains why "empty space" isn't — there is always at least the
field, idling at its lowest energy, ready to ring if disturbed.

A small price for these gains: most of our intuitions about *things* —
that they have edges, locations, and an unambiguous identity — turn out
to be parochial. They were built for medium-sized animals on a planet's
surface, not for the grain of the world.
