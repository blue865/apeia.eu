There is a small honesty in pointing a camera at something that has been
there for ten billion years and will be there long after we are not.

Astrophotography is, at base, a slow practice. You set up gear in the dark,
you wait for the sky to behave, you stack thousands of seconds of light
and hope a faint structure emerges. None of it is fast and none of it
scales. In a world that has decided fast and scalable are virtues, this is
the appeal.

The other appeal is humility. Every serious deep-sky target is older than
the human story; most are older than the Earth. To work with that
timescale is to be reminded — gently, repeatedly — that the things we are
anxious about today will not, on the longest views, matter.

This is not nihilism. It is, I think, the opposite. If the universe does
not hand us meaning, the meaning we make is unambiguously ours. We are
the part of the cosmos that looks back at it. Doing that well, and
reporting honestly what we find, seems like a reasonable use of a life.
